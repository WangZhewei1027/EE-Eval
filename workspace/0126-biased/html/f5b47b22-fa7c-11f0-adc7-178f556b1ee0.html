What are Digital Signatures?

Digital signatures are a method of verifying the authenticity and integrity of digital documents. They use a unique code, known as a hash, to ensure that the document has not been tampered with or altered during transmission.

How do Digital Signatures Work?

Digital signatures use a combination of cryptography and hashing algorithms to create a unique digital signature for a document. The process involves the following steps:

  • Sign the document using a private key and a hash function.
  • Store the signed document and the private key in a secure location.
  • Use the public key to verify the digital signature.

The Algorithm

The most common digital signature algorithm used is the RSA algorithm. It works by using a large prime number to create a public key and a private key. The public key is used to sign the document, while the private key is used to verify the digital signature.

The RSA algorithm uses the following steps:

  • Choose two large prime numbers, p and q.
  • Compute the modulus n = p * q.
  • Choose a public exponent e, which is typically 65537.
  • Compute the private exponent d, which is the modular inverse of e modulo (p-1) (p-1)/e.
  • Compute the digital signature s = m^e mod n, where m is the message to be signed.
  • Return the digital signature s.

Example

Suppose we want to sign a document with the message "Hello, World!" using the RSA algorithm with a public key and a modulus of 101 and 103.

First, we choose a prime number p = 101 and a prime number q = 103. We then compute the modulus n = p * q = 10403.

Next, we choose a public exponent e = 65537. We then compute the private exponent d, which is the modular inverse of e modulo (p-1) (p-1)/e. In this case, d = 25.

Now, we can compute the digital signature s = m^e mod n, where m = "Hello, World!". We get s = m^e mod n = "Hello, World!"^65537 mod 10403 = "Hello, World!"^2575 = "Hello, World!".

Verification

To verify the digital signature, we use the public key to compute the digital signature s = m^e mod n, where m is the original message. If the two signatures match, then the document has not been tampered with or altered during transmission.

In our example, the digital signature s = "Hello, World!"^2575 matches the original message m = "Hello, World!". This confirms that the document has not been tampered with or altered during transmission.

Conclusion

Digital signatures provide a secure way to verify the authenticity and integrity of digital documents. They use a unique code, known as a hash, to ensure that the document has not been tampered with or altered during transmission. The RSA algorithm is a widely used digital signature algorithm that is secure and reliable.
