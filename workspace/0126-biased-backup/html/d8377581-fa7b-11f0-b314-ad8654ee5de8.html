1. What is amortized analysis?

Amortized analysis is a technique for finding the average running time per operation over a worst-case sequence of operations. Unlike ordinary average-case analysis (which assumes a distribution over inputs), amortized analysis guarantees that over any sequence of operations, the average cost per operation is bounded by the amortized cost.

In plain language: some operations may be expensive occasionally, but amortized analysis shows that when those expensive operations occur infrequently relative to cheap operations, the average cost per operation is small. It gives a deterministic guarantee about sequences of operations.

Key idea

Consider n operations with actual costs c1, c2, ..., cn. If the total cost is T = Σ ci, then the amortized cost per operation is T/n. Amortized analysis uses clever accounting to bound T for any sequence of operations (not a probability-based average).

2. Why amortized analysis — and how it differs from other analyses

  • Worst-case analysis bounds the cost of a single operation (or the worst possible input). It does not consider that some costly operations may be compensated by many cheap ones.
  • Average-case analysis computes the expected cost assuming a probability distribution over inputs or operations. It depends on assumptions about randomness.
  • Amortized analysis bounds the average cost per operation over any sequence of operations — there is no probability distribution. It produces a guarantee for sequences, and is often more informative for dynamic data structures (e.g., dynamic arrays, splay trees, Fibonacci heaps).
Important: Amortized bounds do not imply every operation is cheap. Rather they guarantee that expensive operations are infrequent enough that the sequence average is small.

3. Three principal methods for amortized analysis

There are three standard approaches. Each proves the same kind of result but offers different intuition.

3.1 Aggregate method

Compute the total cost T(n) for n operations in the worst case, then divide by n. If you can show T(n) ≤ f(n), then the amortized cost per operation is f(n)/n.

When to use: When you can sum up total costs easily (e.g., count how many times an expensive action can happen across n operations).

3.2 Accounting (or banker) method

Assign each operation an amortized charge (a number of "dollars" or credits) that may be larger than its actual cost. Surplus charged money is stored as credit on data-structure components and used to pay for future expensive operations. You must ensure two constraints:

  • The total charged money over the sequence ≥ the total actual cost (i.e., you never go into debt).
  • Credits stored are never negative and represent real prepayments for future work.

3.3 Potential method

Define a potential function Φ mapping each state of the data structure to a nonnegative real number (Φ ≥ 0). The amortized cost for an operation that takes the structure from state S_before to S_after is:

amortized_cost = actual_cost + Φ(S_after) − Φ(S_before)

Summing over a sequence, telescoping cancels intermediate potentials, leaving:

Σ amortized_cost = Σ actual_cost + Φ(final) − Φ(initial)

If Φ(initial) = 0 and Φ(final) ≥ 0, then Σ amortized_cost ≥ Σ actual_cost, so amortized costs upper-bound the total actual cost. Carefully choosing Φ yields tight bounds and formal proofs.

4. Worked examples

4.1 Dynamic array (append with doubling)

Consider an array that supports append. Start with an array of capacity 1. When the array is full and we append, we allocate a new array of twice the capacity and copy all existing elements into it, then append the new element.

Actual costs: Most appends cost O(1) (just store element). When a resize happens at size k, the operation pays cost k (to copy k items) plus 1 to insert; we may model the resize operation's cost as k+1 or simply k with bookkeeping. For amortized analysis we count copies and inserts precisely as needed.

Aggregate method (dynamic array)

Suppose you perform n appends starting from empty array and doubling strategy. How many element copies happen in total during all resizes?

Every element, once inserted, may be copied at most O(log n) times? Let's be precise:

  • When you double capacity (1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → ...), each element gets copied each time its containing array is resized.
  • An element inserted when the capacity was 2^i will be copied in subsequent resizes at most (log n − i) times. Summing across elements yields total copies ≤ n + (n/2) + (n/4) + ... < 2n.

Conclusion: Total work for n appends is O(n). Therefore amortized cost per append is O(1).

Accounting method (dynamic array)

Charge each append two units (2 "dollars"):

  • Use 1 dollar to pay for the actual insertion.
  • Store 1 dollar as credit on the newly inserted element.
When a resize occurs and we copy k elements to the new array, each of those k elements contributes 1 dollar of stored credit to pay for its copy. So the credits exactly pay for copies during resizes. Hence amortized cost ≤ 2 per append (O(1)).

Potential method (dynamic array)

Define Φ = 2 ⋅ (#elements) − (#capacity). Alternatively, there are multiple valid choices. Intuitively, when the array is half-full or less, potential is small; when it's full, potential is large enough to pay for the copy.

With a careful choice you can show amortized cost per append ≤ 3 (O(1)). The potential increases when inserting without resizing (building credit) and decreases when a resize occurs; the decrease finances the expensive copy.

4.2 Binary counter

Suppose you have a binary counter stored in an array of bits. Increment operation flips bits: you flip trailing 1s to 0s until you flip a 0 to 1. The cost equals 1 + (number of trailing 1 bits). In the worst case (counter = 111...1), increment costs Θ(b) where b is the number of bits.

Aggregate analysis: If you perform n increments starting from 0, how many bit flips occur total? Each time a bit flips from 0 to 1, it must eventually flip from 1 to 0 before it can flip to 1 again. So each bit flips from 0→1 at most ⌈n/2⌉ times and from 1→0 at most ⌈n/2⌉ times, but more cleanly: the total number of flips over n increments is ≤ 2n. Thus the amortized cost per increment is O(1).

Accounting method: Charge $2 per increment: pay 1 for the 0→1 flip, and store 1 credit to pay for the eventual 1→0 flip. Credits accumulate appropriately. So amortized cost O(1).

4.3 Stack with multipop

A stack supports push and pop. A multipop(k) pops up to k items. If the stack has m items and k=m, multipop costs O(m). But over a sequence of pushes and pops, each element can be popped at most once per push. Aggregate analysis shows that n operations (push/pop/multipop) cost O(n) total, so amortized O(1) per operation.

5. Formal definitions and theorem statements

Definition (informal)

Given a sequence of n operations with actual costs c1, c2, ..., cn, a set of amortized costs â1, â2, ..., ân is valid if for every possible sequence the sum of amortized costs upper-bounds the sum of actual costs (possibly plus an initial potential): Σ âi ≥ Σ ci (with suitable potential accounting). Often we give a single number α such that the amortized cost per operation is ≤ α.

Potential method formalism

Let D_i denote the data-structure state after i operations (D_0 initial state). Let Φ be a real-valued potential function with Φ(D_0) = 0 and Φ(D_i) ≥ 0 for all i. Define amortized cost for operation i as:

â_i = c_i + Φ(D_i) − Φ(D_{i−1})

Summing i=1..n:

Σ â_i = Σ c_i + Φ(D_n) − Φ(D_0) ≥ Σ c_i

Thus the total actual cost is bounded by the total amortized cost. If you show each â_i ≤ α (constant), then the total cost for n operations is O(α n).

6. Typical pitfalls and clarifications

  • Amortized guarantees apply to sequences. They don't guarantee any particular single operation is cheap.
  • Amortized analysis is not probabilistic; it provides deterministic bounds for any sequence.
  • Choosing a poor potential function may complicate or obscure the analysis, but many natural choices work well (e.g., unused capacity, number of ones in a counter).
  • Amortized analysis often complements worst-case analysis: you can prove that a sequence of n operations costs O(n) while individual operations may cost Θ(n) in the worst case.

7. Practical guidance

  • Use aggregate method when you can count how many times an expensive event can happen across n operations.
  • Use accounting method for intuitive bookkeeping — assign credits to operations and to pieces of data.
  • Use potential method for rigorous proofs and when the stateful view of the data structure helps (it often does for amortized algorithms with complex state like Fibonacci heaps).
  • When writing code, document the amortized complexity to set expectations for performance (e.g., "append amortized O(1)").
  • Remember constants matter in practice: "amortized O(1)" might still have a small constant factor (e.g., charge 2 or 3 credits per operation).

8. Exercises (with solutions)

Exercise 1: Suppose you have a dynamic array that increases capacity by 1 (linear growth) instead of doubling. If you perform n appends starting from capacity 1 and increasing capacity by 1 each time the array is full, what is the total cost and amortized cost per append?
Solution: If capacity increases by 1, then on the k-th resize you copy k items. Total copies up to n equals 1+2+...+n = Θ(n^2). Therefore total cost = Θ(n^2) and amortized cost per append = Θ(n). This is why doubling (geometric growth) is preferred — it gives amortized O(1).
Exercise 2: Show by accounting method that binary counter increments have amortized cost ≤ 2.
Solution: Charge 2 credits per increment. Use 1 credit to pay for the 0→1 flip. Store the other credit on that bit as a reserve to pay for its eventual 1→0 flip. Each flip from 1→0 is paid by the stored credit. Thus 2 credits suffice per increment.
