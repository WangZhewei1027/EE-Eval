Agile Methodology

A software development methodology that emphasizes flexibility, collaboration, and continuous improvement.

Developed in the 1990s by a group of software developers, Agile is an iterative and incremental approach to developing software.

It is based on the principles of Scrum, which emphasizes teamwork, accountability, and iterative progress toward well-defined goals.

Key characteristics of Agile include:

  • Iterative and incremental development: Break down work into small, manageable chunks, and deliver working software in short iterations.
  • Flexibility and adaptability: Be prepared to change direction or adjust the plan if circumstances change or new requirements arise.
  • Collaboration and teamwork: Work together with stakeholders, customers, and team members to understand requirements and deliver software.
  • Continuous improvement: Regularly review and refine the development process to improve efficiency and quality.

Agile is not a one-size-fits-all approach. It can be applied in various contexts, such as software development, product management, and research.

Benefits of Agile include:

  • Improved collaboration: Agile fosters strong relationships between team members, stakeholders, and customers.
  • Increased flexibility: Agile allows for rapid changes and adaptations in response to changing requirements.
  • Enhanced quality: Agile emphasizes continuous testing and refinement, leading to higher-quality software.
  • Better resource allocation: Agile helps to allocate resources more effectively, as needs can change quickly.

Scrum Framework

Scrum is a specific Agile framework that emphasizes teamwork, accountability, and iterative progress toward well-defined goals.

It consists of three roles:

  • Product Owner: Responsible for defining and prioritizing the product backlog.
  • Scrum Master: Facilitates the Scrum process, removes impediments, and ensures adherence to Scrum principles.
  • Development Team: Responsible for developing the software.

Scrum ceremonies include:

  • Sprint Planning: A meeting to plan and prioritize the work for the upcoming sprint.
  • Daily Scrum: A meeting to review progress, discuss obstacles, and plan the next day.
  • Sprint Review: A meeting to review and demonstrate the working software.
  • Sprint Retrospective: A meeting to review and improve the Scrum process.

Kanban Board

Kanban is a visual system for managing work, emphasizing continuous flow and limiting work in progress.

It consists of four columns:

  • To-Do: New work that needs to be done.
  • In Progress: Work in progress that is currently being developed.
  • Done: Completed work that is ready to be delivered.
  • Waiting On: Work waiting on resources or information.

Kanban is a flexible and adaptable system, allowing teams to adjust their workflow as needed.
